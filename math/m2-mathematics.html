1. 2D Differential Drive Kinematics

The TurtleBot4 Lite uses a differential drive base (iRobot Create3). The robot's pose in the plane is fully described by three quantities, and motion is commanded by the independent angular velocities of the left and right wheels.

1.1 State Vector and Control Inputs

Eq. 1 — State and control
$$\mathbf{q}(t) = \begin{bmatrix} x(t) \\ y(t) \\ \psi(t) \end{bmatrix}, \qquad \mathbf{u}(t) = \begin{bmatrix} \omega_L(t) \\ \omega_R(t) \end{bmatrix}$$
SymbolMeaningUnits
$x,\,y$Position of robot centre in world framem
$\psi$Heading angle from positive $x$-axisrad
$\omega_L,\,\omega_R$Left/right wheel angular velocitiesrad s⁻¹

1.2 Wheel Velocities → Linear and Angular Velocity

Each wheel of radius $r$ contributes a tangential speed. The net forward speed $v$ and yaw rate $\dot\psi$ are their symmetric and antisymmetric combinations, normalised by wheelbase $L$:

Eq. 2 — Forward speed
$$v(t) = \frac{r}{2}\bigl(\omega_R(t) + \omega_L(t)\bigr)$$
Eq. 3 — Yaw rate
$$\dot{\psi}(t) = \frac{r}{L}\bigl(\omega_R(t) - \omega_L(t)\bigr)$$
SymbolMeaningTurtleBot4 Lite value
$r$Wheel radius0.0352 m
$L$Track width (wheel-to-wheel)0.233 m
$v$Forward linear speedmax 0.31 m/s
$\dot\psi$Yaw ratemax 1.90 rad/s

1.3 Continuous-Time Motion Model

Projecting the forward speed onto the world frame gives the full nonlinear kinematic model:

Eq. 4 — Kinematic ODE
$$\dot{\mathbf{q}}(t) = \begin{bmatrix} \dot{x}(t) \\ \dot{y}(t) \\ \dot{\psi}(t) \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} v(t)\cos\psi(t) \\ v(t)\sin\psi(t) \\ \dot{\psi}(t) \end{bmatrix} = f\bigl(\mathbf{q}(t),\,\mathbf{u}(t)\bigr)$$

1.4 Discrete-Time State Update (Euler Integration)

For a fixed timestep $\Delta t$ using forward Euler:

Eq. 5 — Euler step
$$\mathbf{q}_{k+1} = \mathbf{q}_k + \Delta t \cdot f(\mathbf{q}_k,\,\mathbf{u}_k)$$
Eq. 6 — Expanded components
$$\begin{aligned} x_{k+1} &= x_k + v_k \cos\psi_k \cdot \Delta t \\ y_{k+1} &= y_k + v_k \sin\psi_k \cdot \Delta t \\ \psi_{k+1} &= \psi_k + \dot{\psi}_k \cdot \Delta t \end{aligned}$$

1.5 Exact Arc-Based Integration

When $\dot\psi_k \neq 0$, integrating exactly over the arc eliminates Euler accumulation error. Let $\kappa = \dot\psi_k\,\Delta t$:

Eq. 7 — Arc integration
$$\begin{aligned} x_{k+1} &= x_k + \frac{v_k}{\dot{\psi}_k}\Bigl(\sin(\psi_k+\kappa) - \sin\psi_k\Bigr) \\ y_{k+1} &= y_k - \frac{v_k}{\dot{\psi}_k}\Bigl(\cos(\psi_k+\kappa) - \cos\psi_k\Bigr) \\ \psi_{k+1} &= \psi_k + \kappa \end{aligned}$$
Note: When $\dot\psi_k \approx 0$ (straight-line motion), Eq. 6 is numerically preferred. Eq. 7 degenerates gracefully via L'Hôpital's rule to the same result.
Eq. 8 — Full mapping: wheel velocities → state update
$$\begin{bmatrix} x_{k+1} \\ y_{k+1} \\ \psi_{k+1} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} x_k \\ y_k \\ \psi_k \end{bmatrix} + \frac{r\,\Delta t}{2} \begin{bmatrix} (\omega_{R,k}+\omega_{L,k})\cos\psi_k \\ (\omega_{R,k}+\omega_{L,k})\sin\psi_k \\ \dfrac{2}{L}(\omega_{R,k}-\omega_{L,k}) \end{bmatrix}$$

2. Frontier Scoring Formula

Among all candidate frontier regions $R$ on the boundary between known-free and unknown space, the robot selects the one with the highest score. This balances three competing objectives: reward large frontiers, reward high information gain, and penalise distant targets.

2.1 The Formula

Eq. 9 — Frontier score
$$\text{score}(R) = \frac{\alpha \cdot \text{size}(R) \;+\; \beta \cdot \text{unknown}(R)}{\text{dist}(R) + \varepsilon}$$
SymbolMeaningValue in code
$\text{size}(R)$Number of frontier cells in region $R$cluster size
$\text{unknown}(R)$Unknown cells within $r=0.5$ m of centroidcount at radius
$\text{dist}(R)$Euclidean distance from robot to centroidmetres
$\alpha$Size weight1.0
$\beta$Information gain weight2.0
$\varepsilon$Division-by-zero guard1e-6
Correction from draft: The original Part 1 draft suggested α ∈ [0.3, 1.0] and β ∈ [1.0, 3.0]. Part 2 inconsistently used α ∈ [0.3, 0.5], β ∈ [0.5, 0.7]. The values actually implemented are α = 1.0, β = 2.0, ε = 1×10⁻⁶, chosen to weight information gain twice as heavily as frontier size.

2.2 Parameter Choices and Rationale

α — size weight (1.0)

Favours large open frontiers. Prevents the robot from wasting navigation budget on tiny isolated pockets. Typical range [0.3, 1.0].

β — information gain weight (2.0)

Directly rewards unexplored area. Set to 2× size weight to drive aggressive coverage. Higher β produces faster but noisier exploration. Range [1.0, 3.0].

ε — stability guard (1e-6)

Must satisfy $\varepsilon \ll \min_R \text{dist}(R)$. At 1×10⁻⁶ m it stabilises the denominator without biasing scores for any realistic robot position.

α/β ratio

Only the ratio matters — scaling both by a constant leaves the ranking unchanged. Our ratio α/β = 0.5 means the robot prioritises information density over raw frontier area.

2.3 Failure Modes and Mitigations

Failure ModeCauseMitigation
Greedy cyclingTwo frontiers with equal scores cause oscillationHysteresis bonus for currently-targeted frontier
Distance dominanceLate in mapping all frontiers are far — denominator overwhelms numeratorNormalise dist by map diagonal or use log penalty
Wrong α/β for environmentOpen warehouses vs tight corridors need different tuningTune offline on representative map; adapt to local occupancy variance
ε too largeDistance penalty vanishes for near frontiersKeep ε ≪ minimum expected distance

3. SLAM — Occupancy Grid via Bayes Rule

SLAM Toolbox maintains a probabilistic occupancy grid where each cell $m_i$ stores the log-odds of being occupied given all sensor observations $z_{1:t}$.

3.1 Log-Odds Representation

The log-odds of a probability $p$ is defined as:

Eq. 10 — Log-odds
$$l(p) = \log\left(\frac{p}{1-p}\right)$$

This maps probabilities $[0,1]$ to $(-\infty, +\infty)$, making Bayesian updates an additive operation that is numerically stable and computationally cheap.

3.2 Recursive Bayesian Update

Eq. 11 — Log-odds update rule
$$l(m_i \mid z_{1:t}) = l(m_i \mid z_{1:t-1}) + l(m_i \mid z_t) - l_0$$
TermMeaningValue (SLAM Toolbox default)
$l(m_i \mid z_{1:t})$Posterior log-odds after observation $z_t$—
$l(m_i \mid z_t)$Sensor model: log-odds from single scan hit/misshit: +0.85 · miss: −0.4
$l_0$Prior log-odds (uniform prior = 50% occupied)0

To recover probability from log-odds: $p = 1 - \dfrac{1}{1 + e^l}$. The OccupancyGrid message encodes these probabilities as integers 0–100 (0 = free, 100 = occupied, −1 = unknown).

4. Extended Kalman Filter — State Estimation

The robot_localization EKF node fuses wheel odometry (/odom) and IMU data to produce /odometry/filtered. The EKF linearises the nonlinear motion model at each step to propagate the Gaussian uncertainty estimate.

4.1 Prediction Step

Using the kinematic model from Section 1, the predicted state and covariance are:

Eq. 12 — State prediction
$$\hat{\mathbf{q}}_{k+1|k} = \mathbf{q}_k + \Delta t \begin{bmatrix} v\cos\theta_k \\ v\sin\theta_k \\ \frac{r}{L}(\omega_R - \omega_L) \end{bmatrix}$$
Eq. 13 — Covariance prediction
$$\mathbf{P}_{k+1|k} = \mathbf{F}_k \mathbf{P}_k \mathbf{F}_k^\top + \mathbf{Q}_k$$

where the Jacobian of the motion model $\mathbf{F}_k$ (first-order Taylor linearisation) is:

Eq. 14 — Motion Jacobian
$$\mathbf{F}_k = \mathbf{I} + \Delta t \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 0 & -v\sin\theta_k \\ 0 & 0 & v\cos\theta_k \\ 0 & 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$
SymbolMeaning
$\mathbf{P}_k$3×3 state covariance matrix at step $k$
$\mathbf{Q}_k$Process noise covariance (wheel slip, unmodelled dynamics)
$\mathbf{F}_k$Jacobian of $f$ with respect to state $\mathbf{q}$

4.2 Update Step

When a sensor measurement $z_k$ arrives (e.g. IMU yaw rate, wheel encoder), the Kalman gain and corrected state are:

Eq. 15 — Kalman gain
$$\mathbf{K}_k = \mathbf{P}_{k|k-1}\mathbf{H}_k^\top \left(\mathbf{H}_k \mathbf{P}_{k|k-1} \mathbf{H}_k^\top + \mathbf{R}_k \right)^{-1}$$
Eq. 16 — State update
$$\hat{\mathbf{q}}_{k|k} = \hat{\mathbf{q}}_{k|k-1} + \mathbf{K}_k \left(z_k - h(\hat{\mathbf{q}}_{k|k-1})\right)$$
Eq. 17 — Covariance update
$$\mathbf{P}_{k|k} = (\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{K}_k \mathbf{H}_k)\mathbf{P}_{k|k-1}$$
SymbolMeaning
$\mathbf{H}_k$Jacobian of measurement model $h$ w.r.t. state
$\mathbf{R}_k$Measurement noise covariance
$\mathbf{K}_k$Kalman gain — weights prediction vs measurement trust
$z_k - h(\hat{\mathbf{q}})$Innovation: difference between actual and predicted measurement
Connection to our system: The EKF output /odometry/filtered is consumed by both the frontier explorer node (robot position for scoring) and the behavior coordinator node (proximity checks for visited frontier filtering).

5. Semantic Hazard Classifier

The classifier operates on HSV colour space patches extracted from the OAK-D RGB stream. For each $3 \times 4$ patch grid, three conditions are evaluated: hue range, saturation, and edge density.

5.1 HSV Threshold Rules

WATER$90° \lt H \lt 130°$$S \gt 20$$V \gt 80$—$> 18\%$ of patch

5.2 Edge Density Formula

For a patch region-of-interest (ROI), the edge density $\rho$ is computed using the Canny edge detector with thresholds $\tau_1 = 50$, $\tau_2 = 150$:

Eq. 18 — Edge density
$$\rho_{\text{edge}} = \frac{\left|\left\{p \in \text{ROI} : |\nabla I(p)| > \tau\right\}\right|}{|\text{ROI}|}$$

where $|\nabla I(p)|$ is the gradient magnitude at pixel $p$ computed by the Canny operator. Debris, cables, and structural collapse produce $\rho > 0.22$; smooth clean floors produce $\rho < 0.10$.

5.3 HAZMAT Detection — YOLOv3-tiny and Non-Maximum Suppression

DeepHAZMAT uses a YOLOv3-tiny backbone that outputs bounding box predictions $(t_x, t_y, t_w, t_h, p_o)$ per grid cell per anchor. The predicted box centre is:

Eq. 19 — YOLO bounding box decoding
$$b_x = \sigma(t_x) + c_x, \quad b_y = \sigma(t_y) + c_y, \quad b_w = p_w e^{t_w}, \quad b_h = p_h e^{t_h}$$

where $(c_x, c_y)$ is the grid cell offset, $(p_w, p_h)$ are the prior anchor dimensions, and $\sigma$ is the sigmoid function. After decoding, Adaptive Non-Maximum Suppression (A-NMS) removes redundant overlapping boxes:

Eq. 20 — IoU for NMS
$$\text{IoU}(A,B) = \frac{|A \cap B|}{|A \cup B|}$$

Boxes with $\text{IoU} > \tau_{\text{NMS}} = 0.3$ and lower confidence are suppressed. Our minimum confidence threshold is set to $p_o > 0.5$ to reduce false positives in cluttered disaster-zone environments.

6. Nav2 DWA Local Planner

The Dynamic Window Approach (DWA) samples feasible velocity commands and selects the one that best balances goal heading, obstacle clearance, and forward speed.

6.1 Velocity Constraints

The dynamic window restricts commands to velocities reachable within one control cycle $\Delta t$ given current acceleration limits:

Eq. 21 — Dynamic window
$$v \in \bigl[v_c - a_v \Delta t,\; v_c + a_v \Delta t\bigr], \qquad \omega \in \bigl[\omega_c - a_\omega \Delta t,\; \omega_c + a_\omega \Delta t\bigr]$$

These are further clipped to the robot's absolute limits $v \in [v_{\min}, v_{\max}]$, $\omega \in [-\omega_{\max}, \omega_{\max}]$.

A safety constraint prevents commanding velocities that cannot brake before hitting an obstacle at distance $d_{\text{obstacle}}$:

Eq. 22 — Braking constraint
$$v \leq \sqrt{2 \cdot d_{\text{obstacle}} \cdot a_{v,\text{brake}}}$$

6.2 Objective Function

Eq. 23 — DWA cost
$$G(v,\omega) = \sigma\!\left(\alpha \cdot \text{heading}(v,\omega) + \beta \cdot \text{clearance}(v,\omega) + \gamma \cdot \text{velocity}(v,\omega)\right)$$

where $\sigma$ is a normalisation factor. The three sub-costs are: heading — alignment with the goal direction; clearance — distance to nearest obstacle; velocity — forward speed reward. Nav2's DWB (Dynamic Window Based) controller extends this with configurable critic plugins.

The arc-based trajectory prediction used during velocity sampling follows the same exact integration as Eq. 7:

Eq. 24 — Arc trajectory prediction
$$\begin{bmatrix} x_{k+1} \\ y_{k+1} \\ \theta_{k+1} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} x_k + R\bigl(\sin(\theta_k + \Delta\theta) - \sin\theta_k\bigr) \\ y_k - R\bigl(\cos(\theta_k + \Delta\theta) - \cos\theta_k\bigr) \\ \theta_k + \Delta\theta \end{bmatrix}, \qquad R = \frac{b}{2} \cdot \frac{\omega_R + \omega_L}{\omega_R - \omega_L}$$

7. 3D Differential Drive Kinematics (Uneven Terrain)

For completeness — and for future M3 hardware testing on uneven surfaces — the 2D model extends to 6-DOF using ZYX Euler angles. Control inputs remain the two wheel velocities; $z$, roll, and pitch are constrained by the terrain.

Eq. 25 — 3D state vector
$$\mathbf{q}(t) = \begin{bmatrix} x(t)\\y(t)\\z(t)\\\phi(t)\\\theta(t)\\\psi(t) \end{bmatrix}, \qquad \mathbf{u}(t) = \begin{bmatrix}\omega_L(t)\\\omega_R(t)\end{bmatrix}$$

The rotation matrix $R(\phi,\theta,\psi)$ using ZYX convention ($R_z(\psi)\,R_y(\theta)\,R_x(\phi)$):

Eq. 26 — Rotation matrix
$$R = \begin{bmatrix} \cos\psi\cos\theta & \cos\psi\sin\theta\sin\phi - \sin\psi\cos\phi & \cos\psi\sin\theta\cos\phi + \sin\psi\sin\phi \\ \sin\psi\cos\theta & \sin\psi\sin\theta\sin\phi + \cos\psi\cos\phi & \sin\psi\sin\theta\cos\phi - \cos\psi\sin\phi \\ -\sin\theta & \cos\theta\sin\phi & \cos\theta\cos\phi \end{bmatrix}$$

World-frame velocity (robot moves along body $x$-axis at speed $v$):

Eq. 27 — 3D translational velocity
$$\begin{bmatrix}\dot x \\ \dot y \\ \dot z\end{bmatrix} = v(t) \begin{bmatrix}\cos\psi\cos\theta \\ \sin\psi\cos\theta \\ -\sin\theta\end{bmatrix}$$

The complete discrete-time update — only $x$, $y$, $\psi$ are numerically integrated; $z$, $\phi$, $\theta$ are read from terrain model $h(x,y)$:

Eq. 28 — 3D state update (boxed)
$$\boxed{\begin{aligned} x_{k+1} &= x_k + v_k\cos\psi_k\cos\theta_k\,\Delta t \\ y_{k+1} &= y_k + v_k\sin\psi_k\cos\theta_k\,\Delta t \\ z_{k+1} &= h(x_{k+1},\,y_{k+1}) \\ \phi_{k+1} &= \phi_{\text{surface}}(x_{k+1},\,y_{k+1},\,\psi_{k+1}) \\ \theta_{k+1} &= \theta_{\text{surface}}(x_{k+1},\,y_{k+1},\,\psi_{k+1}) \\ \psi_{k+1} &= \psi_k + \dot\psi_k\,\Delta t \end{aligned}}$$
Singularity warning: The Euler angle parameterisation is undefined at $\theta = \pm 90°$ (gimbal lock). For steep terrain, replace Euler angles with unit quaternion $\mathbf{q} = (q_0, q_1, q_2, q_3)^\top$ and use $\dot{\mathbf{q}} = \tfrac{1}{2}\,\Xi(\mathbf{q})\,\boldsymbol\omega_b$.
